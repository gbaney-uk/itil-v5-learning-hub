ITIL V5 Four Dimensions of Product and Service Management

Colour mind map for revision: the Four Dimensions provide a balanced, holistic perspective for managing digital products and services.

The Four Dimensions

The Four Dimensions of Product and Service Management represent the critical perspectives that must be considered to ensure effective and efficient facilitation of value for customers and other stakeholders in the form of products and services.

Each dimension is distinct but interdependent, so they must always be considered together.

Introduction / core idea

  • Managing digital products and services requires more than focusing on individual components such as technology or processes.
  • Services exist within complex systems involving people, information, partners and workflows.
  • Focusing on one aspect in isolation can lead to inefficiencies, increased risk and reduced value.

The four dimensions are

  1. Organizations and people
  2. Information and technology
  3. Partners and suppliers
  4. Value streams and processes

They apply universally to all organizations and to both service providers and service consumers.

Purpose of the Four Dimensions

  • Support a holistic approach to product and service management.
  • Help organizations identify gaps, dependencies and risks.
  • Avoid decisions being made from a single viewpoint.
  • Introducing new technology without considering skills and culture may result in poor adoption.

Applying the Four Dimensions

  • Consider them whenever changes are made to products or services.
  • Use them when designing or modifying services.
  • Use them when assessing risks and impacts.
  • They are not a checklist; they guide thinking and discussion.

Relationship with other ITIL concepts

  • The Four Dimensions are not a standalone tool.
  • They influence and support service relationships, governance, the ITIL value system, the service value chain, management practices, value streams and continual improvement.
  • They provide a common lens that can be applied across these areas.

Position within the ITIL framework

  • The Four Dimensions form a foundational concept that supports all other guidance.
  • Understanding them early provides essential context for later topics.
  • They are introduced before exploring how services are structured, governed and improved.

Visual concept

Products and services sit at the centre. Value is supported by the four dimensions and affected by external factors.

Every dimension is affected by multiple factors.

ITIL V5 Four Dimensions: Organisations and People

People, structure, culture, leadership, skills and collaboration must align to create value.

Organisations and People

Core idea: Best technology or process still fails if people factors are not aligned.

Purpose / why it matters

  • Supports a holistic approach to product and service management.
  • Reveals hidden gaps, dependencies and risks.
  • Avoids narrow single-viewpoint decisions.
  • Ignoring people factors can cause resistance, inconsistent delivery and reduced value.

Four Dimensions context

  1. Organizations and people
  2. Information and technology
  3. Partners and suppliers
  4. Value streams and processes

Memory hook: People need direction, trust, structure, skills and communication to create value.

Structure and governance

  • Structure determines how responsibilities, decisions and authority are distributed.
  • Governance clarifies roles, accountability and control.
  • Clear ownership keeps services aligned with organizational objectives.
  • Poor structure causes unclear ownership, slow decisions and fragmented delivery.

Leadership

  • Sets direction and reinforces desired behaviours.
  • Supports people through change.
  • Values-driven and people-centred.
  • Builds trust through integrity, learning and listening.
  • Balances organizational goals with stakeholder wellbeing.

Roles, skills and capabilities

  • People need the right skills and competencies for their roles.
  • They must understand responsibilities and objectives.
  • They need training and development.
  • Capabilities include technical expertise, service management knowledge, communication and cross-discipline collaboration.
  • Insufficient skills or capacity increases risk and limits performance.

Communication and collaboration

  • Coordinates work across teams and organizational boundaries.
  • Creates shared understanding of goals, priorities and constraints.
  • Improves decision-making and reduces friction.
  • Critical where digital work spans multiple functions and organizations.

Culture and safety culture

  • Culture is shared values expressed through behaviour, ideas, beliefs and practices.
  • Culture shapes behaviour, change response and collaboration.
  • Safety culture means people are comfortable being and expressing themselves.
  • People feel trusted, raise risks early and speak up about issues.
  • Supports learning, transparency, improvement and shared responsibility.

Applying the dimension

  • Consider it when designing or modifying services.
  • Use it when assessing risks and impacts.
  • Use it when planning improvements and evaluating performance.
  • It is not a tick-box checklist; it guides thinking and discussion.
  • External PESTLE factors affect every dimension.
Supports other ITIL concepts: Service relationships Governance ITIL value system Service value chain Management practices Value streams Continual improvement

Exam takeaway

  • Do not treat Organisations and People as HR only.
  • The Four Dimensions are a thinking lens, not a simple checklist.
  • Use them whenever products and services are designed, delivered, operated or improved.

ITIL V5 Four Dimensions: Information and Technology

Information, knowledge, technology, automation, AI, security and governance must support value.

Information and Technology

This dimension considers the information and knowledge needed to manage products and services, as well as the technologies required to support them.

Technology only creates value when information is accurate, available, protected, governed and used well.

Introduction / core idea

  • Information and technology are fundamental enablers of digital products and services.
  • Together, they support decision-making, coordination, automation and continual improvement.
  • Technology alone does not create value.
  • Value is enabled when information is accurate, accessible and used effectively with appropriate technologies.

The key concepts are

  1. Data, information and knowledge
  2. Information management and knowledge sharing
  3. Technology as an enabler
  4. Automation and artificial intelligence
  5. Security, reliability and compliance

Purpose of the dimension

  • Ensures organizations consider not only tools and systems, but also the information those systems manage.
  • Helps improve effectiveness, resilience and value creation.
  • Supports sustainable service management by ensuring technology solutions are fit for purpose.
  • Ensures that information enables informed and responsible action.

Data, information and knowledge

  • Data consists of raw facts and observations such as logs, measurements or transaction records.
  • Information is data that has been processed and organized to provide meaning and context.
  • Knowledge is understanding derived from information, combined with experience and insight.
  • Poor data quality or inaccessible information can limit understanding and increase risk.

Technology as an enabler

  • Technologies include workflow systems, inventory systems, knowledge bases and collaboration tools.
  • Cloud platforms, mobile solutions and automated testing are common ways of working.
  • Automation and analytics increase efficiency, consistency and insight.
  • Poorly integrated or difficult tools can reduce value rather than enhance it.

Information management and sharing

  • Information must be managed throughout its lifecycle.
  • This includes ensuring accuracy, timeliness, relevance and accessibility.
  • Knowledge sharing is important in service relationships and operational environments.
  • Making knowledge available to the right people at the right time improves consistency and resilience.

Automation, AI and governance

  • Automation can reduce manual effort and errors.
  • Analytics support informed decision-making by identifying patterns and trends.
  • AI helps organizations manage complexity and automate repetitive work.
  • AI must be governed carefully to manage bias, transparency and reliability.
  • Data governance defines rules, policies, standards, processes and controls for managing data assets.

Security, reliability and compliance

  • This dimension introduces risks that must be managed.
  • Risks include cybersecurity threats, data privacy concerns, system failures and regulatory requirements.
  • Security, reliability and compliance are essential to maintaining trust in products and services.
  • Organizations must protect information, ensure availability and align technology use with legal obligations.
Supports other ITIL concepts: Service relationships Governance ITIL value system Service value chain Management practices Value streams Continual improvement

Exam takeaway

  • Information and Technology is not just about tools.
  • Technology is an enabler, not value by itself.
  • Value depends on accurate, accessible, protected and well-used information.
  • Data tells what happened, information explains meaning and knowledge supports action.

ITIL V5 Four Dimensions: Partners and Suppliers

Sourcing, contracts, collaboration, dependencies, risk and value contribution must align to create value.

Partners and Suppliers

Core idea: Value depends not only on internal performance, but also on how effectively external organizations are integrated into the service landscape.

Purpose / why it matters

  • Digital products and services are delivered through complex ecosystems involving multiple organizations.
  • Partners and suppliers contribute capabilities, resources, technologies and expertise essential to value creation.
  • Ignoring this dimension can cause fragmented services, unclear accountability and unmanaged dependencies.
  • Considering external relationships improves coordination, resilience and alignment.

Four Dimensions context

  1. Organizations and people
  2. Information and technology
  3. Partners and suppliers
  4. Value streams and processes

Memory hook: Right partners, right sourcing, right integration, right value.

Supplier strategy and sourcing decisions

  • Decide which capabilities to develop internally and which to source externally.
  • Sourcing choices are influenced by cost, strategic importance, risk, expertise and flexibility.
  • Some services use standardized supplier offerings; others require close collaboration with strategic partners.
  • Sourcing decisions should align with service objectives and value expectations.

Contracts, partnerships and collaboration

  • Contracts define responsibilities, service levels and obligations between organizations.
  • Contracts help manage risk and provide clarity about expectations.
  • Contracts alone are not enough to ensure effective service delivery.
  • Successful relationships also depend on collaboration, trust and shared goals.
  • Long-term partnerships often enable better integration, innovation and continual improvement.

Integration and dependencies

  • Products and services often depend on contributions from multiple partners and suppliers.
  • Dependencies must be understood and managed to avoid disruption and inefficiencies.
  • Poor integration can cause delays, inconsistent service quality and increased risk.
  • Processes, information flows and technologies should be aligned across organizational boundaries.

Risk, resilience and value contribution

  • Reliance on partners and suppliers introduces risks related to availability, security, compliance and continuity.
  • These risks must be identified and managed as part of service management.
  • Partners and suppliers can enhance value through specialized capabilities, scalability and innovation.
  • Effective relationship management balances risk and opportunity while supporting sustainable value creation.

Applying the dimension

  • Use this dimension when choosing suppliers, designing service ecosystems and planning sourcing models.
  • Use it when reviewing contracts, dependencies, resilience and supplier performance.
  • Consider how partner roles support service outcomes and organizational priorities.
  • It is not only about procurement; it is about how external organizations contribute to design, development, deployment, delivery, support and continual improvement.
Supports other ITIL concepts: Service relationships Governance ITIL value system Service value chain Management practices Value streams Continual improvement

Exam takeaway

  • Partners and Suppliers is not just vendor management.
  • Contracts matter, but service success also depends on trust, integration and shared goals.
  • Manage external relationships so they strengthen resilience, service outcomes and value creation.

ITIL V5 Four Dimensions: Value Streams and Processes

Value creation, flow, coordination, handoffs, measurement, improvement and complexity thinking.

Value Streams and Processes

This dimension focuses on how work flows through the organization to enable value creation.

It emphasizes that value is not created by isolated tasks, teams or systems, but by coordinated activities that span organizational boundaries.

Why this dimension matters

Ignoring the Value Streams and Processes dimension can lead to fragmented work, inefficiencies and lack of alignment between activities and outcomes.

By explicitly considering how workflows and processes support flow, organizations improve coordination and value delivery.

Four Dimensions context

  1. Organizations and people
  2. Information and technology
  3. Partners and suppliers
  4. Value streams and processes

How they relate

Value streams and processes are closely related but serve different purposes.

A value stream provides an end-to-end view of how value is delivered, while processes describe how individual activities within that value stream are performed.

Focusing only on processes without understanding the value stream can result in local optimization that does not improve overall value delivery.

Definition: Value Stream

  • A value stream is a series of steps an organization undertakes to create and deliver products and services to consumers.
  • Value streams describe how value is created from the consumer’s perspective.
  • They start with a trigger, such as a demand or opportunity, and end with the realization of value.
  • Value streams may involve multiple teams, suppliers and technologies working together.

Flow, coordination and handoffs

  • Effective value streams depend on smooth flow of work across teams and organizational boundaries.
  • Poorly managed handoffs, unclear responsibilities or excessive controls can slow down delivery and reduce value.
  • This dimension encourages organizations to look beyond silos and understand how work actually flows.
  • Organizations can identify bottlenecks, reduce delays and improve collaboration.

Definition: Process

  • A process is a set of interrelated or interacting activities that transforms inputs into outputs.
  • Processes focus on how specific types of work are performed.
  • They define roles, responsibilities, activities and controls that ensure work is carried out consistently and effectively.

Measurement and improvement

  • Understanding value streams and processes supports meaningful measurement.
  • Organizations can assess how effectively value is delivered from start to finish.
  • This perspective supports continual improvement.
  • Organizations can prioritize improvements that have the greatest impact on consumer outcomes and value creation.

Complexity thinking

Complexity thinking is an approach to analysis and decision-making based on understanding the various levels of complexity in systems and their context.

  • Ordered contexts are predictable and support established procedures and best practices.
  • Complex contexts are unpredictable and require experimentation, adaptation and learning.
  • Chaotic contexts are crisis situations where immediate action is needed to restore stability.
  • Confused contexts exist where it is unclear which context applies and the first priority is to assess the situation.

Approaches that work well in ordered situations often fail in complex or chaotic ones. ITIL guidance should be adapted to the situation.

ITIL V5 External Factors (PESTLE)

Political, economic, social, technological, legal and environmental factors influence how products and services are designed, delivered, operated and improved.

External Factors (PESTLE)

Products and services do not exist in isolation. They are influenced by a wide range of external factors that can affect how they are designed, delivered, operated and improved.

This dimension ensures organizations consider influences outside their direct control.

Purpose / why it matters

  • Ignoring external factors can lead to non-compliance, increased risk, reduced relevance or missed opportunities.
  • Understanding the external environment helps organizations adapt and create sustainable value.
  • Each factor can influence service requirements, constraints, risks and opportunities.

PESTLE framework

External factors are commonly analyzed using PESTLE:

  1. Political
  2. Economic
  3. Social
  4. Technological
  5. Legal
  6. Environmental

Every dimension is affected by multiple factors.

Political, Economic and Social Factors

  • Political factors include government policies, public sector priorities, geopolitical stability and regulatory direction.
  • Economic factors include market conditions, inflation, interest rates and overall economic stability.
  • Social factors relate to demographic trends, cultural expectations, user behaviour and societal values.

Technological, Legal and Environmental Factors

  • Technological factors include emerging technologies, innovation speed and technology maturity.
  • Legal factors include laws, regulations, standards and contractual obligations.
  • Environmental factors relate to sustainability, climate impact and responsible use of resources.

Why External Factors Matter

  • External factors can influence all other dimensions of product and service management.
  • New regulations may require changes in processes, technology or partner arrangements.
  • Social or technological changes may require new skills or different ways of working.
  • Considering external factors improves resilience, reduces risk and ensures products and services remain relevant and sustainable.

External factors should be considered

  • When designing or changing products and services.
  • When assessing risks and opportunities.
  • During strategic planning and continual improvement.
  • When ensuring internal choices align with the wider environment.

Link to the Four Dimensions

  • External factors can influence all other dimensions of product and service management.
  • Political, economic, social, technological, legal and environmental influences shape services.
  • Organizations should consider influences outside their direct control when managing products and services.
  • Considering external factors improves resilience and supports sustainable value.

Exam takeaway

  • External Factors help organizations understand influences outside their direct control.
  • PESTLE stands for Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal and Environmental.
  • Use external factors when designing or changing products and services, assessing risks and opportunities, and during strategic planning and continual improvement.

Practice Questions

Use these for active recall. Try to answer before revealing the answer.

Question 1

Why should the Four Dimensions be considered together?

Because focusing on one dimension in isolation can create gaps, risks, inefficiencies or poor decisions. The dimensions are interdependent.

Question 2

Why is Organisations and People not just about HR?

Because it includes leadership, culture, safety culture, communication, collaboration, structure, governance, roles, skills and capabilities.

Question 3

Why is Information and Technology not just about tools?

Because it also covers data, information, knowledge, information management, knowledge sharing, automation, AI, governance, security, reliability and compliance.

Question 4

What does Partners and Suppliers cover beyond procurement?

It covers sourcing choices, contracts, partnerships, collaboration, integration, dependencies, risk, resilience and value contribution.

Question 5

What is the difference between a value stream and a process?

A value stream shows the end-to-end flow of value creation. A process describes specific activities performed within that flow.

Question 6

What does PESTLE stand for?

Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal and Environmental.
